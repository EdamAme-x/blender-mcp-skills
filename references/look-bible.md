## Visual hierarchy

- Pick one hero subject per shot.
- Decide what the viewer should notice in the first second.
- Keep secondary reads weaker than the hero in one of these axes: size, contrast, saturation, sharpness, motion.
- If everything is emphasized, nothing is emphasized.

## Value design

- Build the image in value groups before chasing color.
- Separate foreground, hero, and background into readable brightness bands.
- Avoid midtone soup. If the whole image sits in the middle, the image feels indecisive.
- Give the hero a local contrast advantage.

## Color and contrast

- Pick one dominant temperature relationship for the shot: warm hero on cool world, cool hero on warm world, or near-monochrome with one accent.
- Use saturation sparingly. High saturation everywhere reads cheap fast.
- Put the strongest accent color near the narrative focal point.
- Let shadows stay slightly colored instead of crushing everything to neutral black.

## Shape language

- Read the silhouette at thumbnail size.
- Large, medium, and small shapes should all exist. Repeating same-scale details feels noisy.
- Use straight vs curved contrast intentionally.
- Break symmetry unless the shot specifically wants ritual, monumentality, or machine precision.

## Material readability

- Do not judge materials under flat lighting.
- Separate materials by roughness first, color second.
- Give metal, plastic, painted surfaces, glass, cloth, and rubber distinct specular behavior.
- If two materials have the same value, color, and roughness, they are the same material in practice.
- Small breakup beats random noise. Put wear where contact, gravity, heat, or handling would cause it.

## Camera intent

- Choose the lens for emotional effect, not because it is a default.
- Wide lenses amplify depth and energy but punish sloppy edges and scale.
- Longer lenses simplify background clutter and improve subject separation.
- Low `f-stop` is not automatically cinematic. Use it when selective focus supports the story.
- Fisheye should be rare and motivated.

## Scale cues

- Include at least one believable scale anchor: door, handrail, bolts, seams, human proxy, vegetation, known prop size.
- Without scale anchors, even good modeling can feel toy-like.
- Repeating micro-detail without macro structure makes assets look miniature.

## Atmosphere and depth

- Use depth in layers: foreground frame, hero zone, background support.
- Atmospheric falloff should support depth, not hide weak composition.
- Rim light, haze, or negative fill should clarify planes, not decorate them.
- If depth disappears when color is removed, the shot relies too much on hue and not enough on value.

## Finish criteria

- The hero reads at thumbnail size.
- The value grouping survives desaturation.
- The lighting explains form instead of merely making it visible.
- Material classes are distinguishable without labels.
- The lens choice feels motivated by story or product intent.
- Background detail supports the hero instead of competing with it.
